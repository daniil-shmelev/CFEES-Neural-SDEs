{\centering\bfseries\large
Application: RNA Backbone Torsion Forecasting on $\Torus^d$\par}
\medskip

\section{Introduction}

We apply the \cfees{} integrator to train a neural stochastic
differential equation whose state lies on the flat $d$-torus
$\Torus^{d} = \SO(2)^{d}$. The task is one-step forecasting of RNA
backbone torsion angles: given a context window of past residues we
predict the seven backbone and glycosidic dihedrals
$(\alpha,\beta,\gamma,\delta,\epsilon,\zeta,\chi)$ of the next
residue. The torus is a compact, abelian Lie group, so it exercises
the same retraction-based interface as the rest of the \cfees{}
family while remaining the simplest non-trivial Lie group from a
curvature standpoint. The noncommutative claim of the paper is
carried by the companion SPD experiment; this experiment isolates
two \emph{practical} consequences of the reversible adjoint:
a $1.3$--$1.6\times$ reduction in XLA scratch memory at every
torus dimension we tested, and the ability to integrate several
thousand SDE steps where the \texttt{DirectAdjoint}-based \cgtwo{}
fails outright at $n_{\mathrm{steps}} \geq 300$. Forecasting
accuracy is indistinguishable between the two integrators across the
whole sweep.

\section{Model}

Let $\theta_t \in \Torus^{d}$ denote the torsion-angle state at
residue position $t$, with $d = 7k$ for a window of $k$ consecutive
residues per state. The flat torus has Lie algebra $\Reals^{d}$ with
trivial bracket; the exponential map is componentwise wrap modulo
$2\pi$, and BCH on this group reduces to addition. Given a context
window $(\theta_{t-W+1}, \dots, \theta_t)$ summarised by a GRU
encoder into $c \in \Reals^{c_d}$, the forecast is obtained by
integrating
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:sde}
\mathrm{d}\theta_\tau
= f_\theta(\theta_\tau, c)\,\mathrm{d}\tau
+ g_\theta(\theta_\tau, c)\,\mathrm{d}W_\tau,
\qquad \theta_0 = \theta_t,
\end{equation}
on $\Torus^{d}$, where $W_\tau$ is a standard $d$-dimensional
Brownian motion in tangent coordinates. The drift $f_\theta$ and
diffusion $g_\theta$ are multilayer perceptrons whose inputs are
$(\sin\theta, \cos\theta, c)$. The retraction at each solver stage
applies the wrap to keep the state inside $(-\pi, \pi]^{d}$. Training
minimises the wrapped mean squared error
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:loss}
\mathcal{L}(\hat{\theta}, \theta^{\star})
= \frac{1}{d}\sum_{i=1}^{d}
\Big(\arctan_{2}\!\big(\sin(\hat{\theta}_i - \theta^{\star}_i),
\cos(\hat{\theta}_i - \theta^{\star}_i)\big)\Big)^{\!2},
\end{equation}
which is the squared geodesic distance on each $\SO(2)$ factor and is
smooth almost everywhere on $\Torus^{d}$. The integrator choice
controls only the gradient mechanics: \cfees{} is reversible and
trains under \texttt{ReversibleAdjoint}, which reconstructs the
forward path during backpropagation; \cgtwo{} is not reversible and
trains under \texttt{DirectAdjoint}, which lets JAX differentiate the
unrolled forward graph.

\section{Results}

\paragraph{Forecasting accuracy.}
For each $(\text{solver}, n_{\mathrm{steps}})$ cell we train for
eight epochs of Adam ($10^{-3}$, batch $32$) on $6{,}785$ residue
contexts drawn from $23$ chains and evaluate on a held-out test
split of $336$ contexts from $5$ chains. Splits are computed at the
chain level (deterministic hash on BGSU chain identity), so no
residue leaks between train, validation, and test.
Table~\ref{tab:loss} reports the test wrapped MSE at state dimension
$d = 7$. The spread between solvers at any fixed $n_{\mathrm{steps}}$
is smaller than the spread between adjacent rows of the same solver,
i.e.\ within run-to-run training noise.

\paragraph{Compile-time memory.}
For each cell we lower one forward+backward training step via
\texttt{eqx.filter\_jit}, compile it, and read the
\texttt{temp\_size\_in\_bytes} field of the resulting executable's
memory analysis --- the scratch buffer pool that the JIT region
pre-allocates. This
is a clean, deterministic measurement: it excludes weights,
optimiser state, and dataset buffers, so it isolates the memory cost
of the integrator graph itself. Each cell runs in its own Python
subprocess. Figure~\ref{fig:compute} shows the result on two axes.

\begin{figure}[hbt]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{fig_compute.pdf}
\caption{XLA compile-time scratch buffer for one

solvers through \texttt{equinox}'s \texttt{bounded\_while\_loop},
which applies recursive checkpointing internally and gives a

gap between the curves is the constant overhead of the activation
checkpoint that \texttt{DirectAdjoint} carries and \texttt{ReversibleAdjoint}

\texttt{equinox.internal.\_loop.bounded}; \cfees{} reaches at least
$n_{\mathrm{steps}} = 3000$. (b) versus torus dimension at fixed

sitting consistently above \cfees{} by $7$--$18\,\mathrm{MiB}$.}
\label{fig:compute}

\noindent
Two qualitative observations. First, in this configuration neither
adjoint scales linearly in $n_{\mathrm{steps}}$: \texttt{diffrax}
routes fixed-step solvers through \texttt{equinox}'s bounded loop,
which builds the integration loop as a recursively-checkpointed
scan and allocates only $\mathcal{O}(\log n_{\mathrm{steps}})$
tape regardless of adjoint. The naive
\texttt{DirectAdjoint}-vs-\texttt{ReversibleAdjoint} gap is
absorbed into the checkpointer; what survives is a \emph{constant}
overhead per step, which is what Figure~\ref{fig:compute}~(a)
shows. Second, that constant overhead is not zero and grows with
the state dimension: Figure~\ref{fig:compute}~(b) and
Table~\ref{tab:mem} show that across
$d \in \{7, 14, 35, 70, 140, 210, 350\}$, \cgtwo{} requires
$1.29$--$1.58\times$ the scratch of \cfees{}, with the absolute gap
growing from $7\,\mathrm{MiB}$ at $d=7$ to $18\,\mathrm{MiB}$ at
$d=350$.

\paragraph{Reaching long horizons.}
The constant gap of Table~\ref{tab:mem} is a small win on its own.
The qualitative win is at the upper end of the $n_{\mathrm{steps}}$
axis: \cgtwo{} fails with an \texttt{AssertionError} inside
\texttt{equinox.internal.\_loop.bounded} at every $n_{\mathrm{steps}}
\geq 300$ we tested ($\{300, 400, 500, 1000, 2000\}$), while
\cfees{} successfully lowers, compiles, and back-propagates through
every $n_{\mathrm{steps}}$ we tested up to $3000$. The dotted
vertical line in Figure~\ref{fig:compute}~(a) marks this boundary.
When the SDE rollout has to be long --- e.g.\ generative sampling
needing many noise injections to traverse the conformational
manifold, or finer discretisations of an underlying physical process
--- \cfees{} is the only practical choice in our codebase.

\section{Conclusion}

The flat $d$-torus is a clean Lie-group test bench for the practical
characteristics of the \cfees{} family. With the same model, data,
loss, and $n_{\mathrm{steps}}$, the reversible \cfees{} integrator
(i)~matches the test loss of \cgtwo{} at every step count,
(ii)~uses $1.3$--$1.6\times$ less XLA scratch at every torus
dimension tested, and (iii)~scales to at least an order of magnitude
more integration steps before the non-reversible alternative
collapses. Combined with the $\mathrm{SPD}(n)$ result of the
companion experiment, which exercises the noncommutative machinery
and shows the larger $5.6$--$23\times$ memory savings characteristic
of high-dimensional ambient spaces, this gives a complete picture of
where the reversible \cfees{} adjoint helps and how much.

\paragraph{Implementation notes.}
The GRU encoder has hidden size $128$ and projects into
$c \in \Reals^{64}$; the drift and diffusion MLPs are depth $3$ and
$2$ respectively, both width $128$ with $\mathrm{silu}$
activations, and the diffusion MLP applies a final $\mathrm{softplus}$
with bias $-2.0$ and scale $\sigma_W = 0.1$. Step size
$\mathrm{d}t = 0.05$. Representative chains come from the BGSU
non-redundant RNA list at $3.0\,$\AA{} resolution, fetched in mmCIF
format and parsed with \texttt{biotite}; we drop residues spanning
gaps or with any undefined torsion and keep contiguous runs of
$\geq 30$ residues, splitting by deterministic hash on the chain
identity. Training uses Adam at $10^{-3}$, batch $32$, eight
epochs, with best-model selection by validation wrapped MAE.
Memory is measured by lowering one
$\mathtt{filter\_value\_and\_grad}(\mathcal{L})$ JIT region in a
fresh subprocess and reading the executable's
\texttt{temp\_size\_in\_bytes}; this isolates the integrator's
scratch pool from the (identical) weights, optimiser state, and
dataset buffers. All code lives under \texttt{datasets/rna/},
\texttt{models/torus*.py}, \texttt{experiment/train\_rna.py}, and
the sweep / figure scripts in \texttt{scripts/} and \texttt{report/}.
