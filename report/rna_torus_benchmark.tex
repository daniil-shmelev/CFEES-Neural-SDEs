{\centering\bfseries\large
Application: RNA Backbone Torsion Forecasting on $\Torus^d$\par}
\medskip

\section{Introduction}

We apply the \cfees{} integrator to train a neural stochastic
differential equation whose state lies on the flat $d$-torus
$\Torus^{d} = \SO(2)^{d}$. The task is one-step forecasting of RNA
backbone torsion angles: given a context window of past residues we
predict the seven backbone and glycosidic dihedrals
$(\alpha,\beta,\gamma,\delta,\epsilon,\zeta,\chi)$ of the next
residue. The torus is a compact, abelian Lie group, so it exercises
the same retraction-based interface as the rest of the \cfees{}
family while remaining the simplest non-trivial Lie group from a
curvature standpoint. The noncommutative claim of the paper is
carried by the companion SPD experiment; this experiment isolates
three \emph{practical} consequences of the reversible adjoint on a
clean Lie-group test bench. First, \cfees{} lowers and compiles the
forward+backward graph with $15$--$40\,\mathrm{MiB}$ less resident
memory than \cgtwo{} at every configuration we tested. Second,
\cfees{} successfully back-propagates through up to
$n_{\mathrm{steps}} = 3000$ integration steps while
\texttt{DirectAdjoint}-based \cgtwo{} fails outright at
$n_{\mathrm{steps}} \geq 300$. Third, the wall-clock cost of the
reversible adjoint is non-trivial: \cfees{} is $1.10$--$1.37\times$
\emph{faster} than \cgtwo{} at small step counts where
\texttt{ReversibleAdjoint} only touches a cheap backward recursion,
but $1.12$--$1.32\times$ \emph{slower} at $n_{\mathrm{steps}} \geq
100$ where the reverse-path recomputation dominates. Forecasting
accuracy is indistinguishable between the two integrators.

\section{Model}

Let $\theta_t \in \Torus^{d}$ denote the torsion-angle state at
residue position $t$, with $d = 7k$ for a window of $k$ consecutive
residues per state. The flat torus has Lie algebra $\Reals^{d}$ with
trivial bracket; the exponential map is componentwise wrap modulo
$2\pi$, and BCH on this group reduces to addition. Given a context
window $(\theta_{t-W+1}, \dots, \theta_t)$ summarised by a GRU
encoder into $c \in \Reals^{c_d}$, the forecast is obtained by
integrating
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:sde}
\mathrm{d}\theta_\tau
= f_\theta(\theta_\tau, c)\,\mathrm{d}\tau
+ g_\theta(\theta_\tau, c)\,\mathrm{d}W_\tau,
\qquad \theta_0 = \theta_t,
\end{equation}
on $\Torus^{d}$, where $W_\tau$ is a standard $d$-dimensional
Brownian motion in tangent coordinates. The drift $f_\theta$ and
diffusion $g_\theta$ are multilayer perceptrons whose inputs are
$(\sin\theta, \cos\theta, c)$. The retraction at each solver stage
applies the wrap to keep the state inside $(-\pi, \pi]^{d}$. Training
minimises the wrapped mean squared error
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:loss}
\mathcal{L}(\hat{\theta}, \theta^{\star})
= \frac{1}{d}\sum_{i=1}^{d}
\Big(\arctan_{2}\!\big(\sin(\hat{\theta}_i - \theta^{\star}_i),
\cos(\hat{\theta}_i - \theta^{\star}_i)\big)\Big)^{\!2},
\end{equation}
which is the squared geodesic distance on each $\SO(2)$ factor and is
smooth almost everywhere on $\Torus^{d}$. The integrator choice
controls only the gradient mechanics: \cfees{} is reversible and
trains under \texttt{ReversibleAdjoint}, which reconstructs the
forward path during backpropagation; \cgtwo{} is not reversible and
trains under \texttt{DirectAdjoint}, which lets JAX differentiate the
unrolled forward graph.

\section{Results}

\paragraph{Forecasting accuracy.}
For each $(\text{solver}, n_{\mathrm{steps}})$ cell we train for
eight epochs of Adam ($10^{-3}$, batch $32$) on $6{,}785$ residue
contexts drawn from $23$ chains and evaluate on a held-out test
split of $336$ contexts from $5$ chains. Splits are computed at the
chain level (deterministic hash on BGSU chain identity), so no
residue leaks between train, validation, and test.
Table~\ref{tab:loss} reports the test wrapped MSE at state dimension
$d = 7$. The spread between solvers at any fixed $n_{\mathrm{steps}}$
is smaller than the spread between adjacent rows of the same solver,
i.e.\ within run-to-run training noise.

\paragraph{Compile-time memory.}
For each cell we launch a fresh Python subprocess, build the model,
stamp the resident set size (RSS) once before tracing and again
after the JIT cache is warm, and record the delta as the
\emph{compile footprint} of the forward+backward graph for that
particular solver. Running in isolation matters: the XLA compile
cache persists across JIT calls inside one process and would
contaminate later measurements. We also record the per-rep
incremental RSS (maximum RSS observed during $10$ timed reps minus
post-compile RSS) and, as a cross-check, the JIT region's
\texttt{temp\_size\_in\_bytes} from
\texttt{compiled.compiled.memory\_analysis()}. Two separate facts
fall out:

\begin{enumerate}
\item The per-rep incremental RSS is $0.00\,\mathrm{MiB}$ for both
solvers at every cell in the sweep: XLA pre-allocates all working
buffers at compile time, so the runtime loop itself does not grow
process memory.
\item The compile footprint differs by a consistent
$15$--$40\,\mathrm{MiB}$ between the two solvers, with
\texttt{ReversibleAdjoint} always below \texttt{DirectAdjoint}.
\end{enumerate}

Figure~\ref{fig:compute}~(a) plots the compile footprint against
$n_{\mathrm{steps}}$ at fixed $d=7$. Both curves are essentially
flat: \texttt{diffrax} integrates fixed-step solvers through
\texttt{equinox}'s \texttt{bounded\_while\_loop}, which is a
recursively checkpointed scan that allocates only
$\mathcal{O}(\log n_{\mathrm{steps}})$ tape regardless of adjoint.
The naive \texttt{DirectAdjoint}-vs-\texttt{ReversibleAdjoint} gap is
absorbed into the checkpointer; what survives is the $35\,\mathrm{MiB}$
constant offset visible in the plot. \cgtwo{} additionally fails
outright at $n_{\mathrm{steps}} \geq 300$ with an
\texttt{AssertionError} inside \texttt{equinox.internal.\_loop.bounded}
--- the dotted vertical line in the panel marks this boundary ---
while \cfees{} reaches at least $n_{\mathrm{steps}} = 3000$ on the
same model. When the SDE rollout has to be long --- for instance
generative sampling that needs many noise injections to traverse the
conformational manifold, or finer discretisations of an underlying
physical process --- \cfees{} is the only practical choice in our
codebase. Figure~\ref{fig:compute}~(b) plots the same footprint
against torus dimension at fixed $n_{\mathrm{steps}} = 50$. Both
scale roughly linearly in $d$, with \cgtwo{} again sitting
consistently above \cfees{} by $5$--$40\,\mathrm{MiB}$.

\paragraph{Wall clock.}
The rightmost columns of Table~\ref{tab:mem} report mean
forward+backward wall clock per cell. At small step counts
$n_{\mathrm{steps}} \leq 50$ the reversible adjoint is
$1.10$--$1.37\times$ \emph{faster} than the direct adjoint: the
reverse recurrence in the recursively checkpointed bounded loop is
cheap when the recursion is shallow, and the absolute cost is
dominated by Python and JAX overheads that are identical between
solvers. At $n_{\mathrm{steps}} = 100$ and $200$ the picture
inverts: \cfees{} becomes $1.12$ and $1.32\times$ slower, as the
recomputation cost of the reversible backward path starts to
dominate. This is the expected time--memory trade-off of reversible
integrators made concrete: reversibility buys constant memory at
the cost of one extra forward per backward, which only matters
asymptotically. The crossover in our configuration sits between
$50$ and $100$ integration steps; it will move as the per-step
integrand cost changes.

\section{Conclusion}

The flat $d$-torus is a clean Lie-group test bench for the practical
characteristics of the \cfees{} family. With the same model, data,
loss, and $n_{\mathrm{steps}}$, the reversible \cfees{} integrator
matches the test loss of \cgtwo{} at every step count, uses
$15$--$40\,\mathrm{MiB}$ less resident memory after JIT compilation
at every dimension tested, and scales to an order of magnitude more
integration steps before \cgtwo{} collapses. It is \emph{not}
uniformly faster: \cfees{} beats \cgtwo{} on wall clock for
$n_{\mathrm{steps}} \leq 50$ but pays a $1.12$--$1.32\times$
recomputation tax at $n_{\mathrm{steps}} \geq 100$. Combined with
the $\mathrm{SPD}(n)$ companion experiment, which exercises the
noncommutative machinery and shows the larger $5.6$--$23\times$
memory savings characteristic of high-dimensional ambient spaces,
this gives a complete picture of where the reversible \cfees{}
adjoint helps and how much.

\paragraph{Implementation.}
GRU encoder: hidden $128$, output $c \in \Reals^{64}$. Drift and
diffusion MLPs: depth $3$ and $2$, width $128$, $\mathrm{silu}$;
diffusion closes with $\mathrm{softplus}$ at bias $-2.0$ and scale
$\sigma_W = 0.1$. Representative chains come from the BGSU
non-redundant RNA list at $3.0\,$\AA{} resolution, parsed from
mmCIF via \texttt{biotite}; we keep contiguous runs of $\geq 30$
residues with all seven dihedrals defined and split by deterministic
chain hash. Training: Adam $10^{-3}$, batch $32$, $8$ epochs,
best-model by validation wrapped MAE.
